# Style Profile — Chris / Codespud (technical blog)

Built from 9 articles across 2009–2025 in `content/blog`, weighted toward the current voice (2022–2025): AI accessibility audits, TypeScript discriminated unions, common JS mistakes, focus-within, five-steps-to-accessibility, programmer's resolution, plus older 2009 link posts for range. Genre: **technical web-dev blog posts** (accessibility, JS/TS, CSS). A separate, lighter voice shows up in old 2009 personal/link posts — noted but not the focus.

---

## 1. Voice in one line

A self-deprecating senior front-end dev who teaches by confessing his own mistakes, keeps sentences short and plain, then sneaks in a pop-culture punchline and signs off with "Happy coding!"

## 2. Distinctive fingerprints (the 3 that matter most)

1. **Confessional teaching.** He frames lessons as his own failures, not lectures. Titles and lines like "Five common Javascript mistakes **I am guilty of**," "I feel foolish whenever I commit this next error," "I thought I had been doing a great job... **I was dead wrong**," and the 2009 title "**M I STUPID?**" The reader is a peer, not a student.

2. **Plain short sentences, then a wisecrack.** The technical body is deliberately flat and declarative — "Errors in code are normal." "Accessibility is very important." — and the personality lives in parenthetical asides and analogies: "(Spoiler: it's always missing.)", "like searching for a semicolon in a haystack," "about as helpful as a screen door on a submarine."

3. **Pop-culture analogies to explain code.** Especially 2024–2025: discriminated unions are "the Avengers assembling," "a Hogwarts sorting hat," "your own tech-savvy Justice League," "a Swiss Army knife of commands," self-documenting "like a well-curated 80's playlist." Code gets a cape.

## 3. Structure & format

- **Listicle by default.** Numbered "N things" is the house format: "Five common...", "Ten common... Examples", "Five simple steps", "Five... resolutions." When in doubt, he counts.
- **Open with a hook, not a preamble.** A blunt confession or a "let's be honest" framing: "Let's be honest: accessibility is one of those things that everyone agrees is important, but somehow always ends up on the 'we'll get to it later' list." / "I am the most undisciplined person I know." / "Errors in code are normal."
- **Anchor table of contents** near the top of longer posts — a bulleted list of in-page links (`./#equality`, `#frameworks`).
- **Close with a "Wrap-Up" / "Wrapping up" heading**, a one-line takeaway, then a sign-off (see §6). Bookends an epigraph: the AI post opens *and* closes on a pulled quote.
- **Code-block driven.** Most sections are short prose → fenced code block → short prose. Heavy use of `// Before` / `// suggestion` comments inside the code to carry the lesson.
- **Italicized commentary** right under a code block to explain it (the whole TS post runs this rhythm).
- Paragraphs are **short — 2 to 4 sentences.** He rarely writes a block longer than ~5 lines of prose before breaking to code, a list, or a heading.

## 4. Sentence & rhythm mechanics

- Average sentence is short, ~12–16 words, subject-verb-object, present tense.
- **Fragments for emphasis**, often as rhetorical questions: "But nuance? Context?" / "Big sites and frequent updates? No sweat." / "Do you see my error?"
- **Direct second-person address**: "If you're a developer or DevOps engineer, you know the drill." / "be careful and make sure you know what you're doing."
- **Colons and em-dashes** are his two favorite connectors: "let's face it—they're slow, expensive..." / "here's the thing:".
- First person singular **"I"** throughout — personal experience, not the editorial "we" (he does use "we" for shared walk-throughs: "We can fix this in a few ways").

## 5. Diction & vocabulary

- Casual register: "you know the drill," "no sweat," "get away with," "oops moments," "trip most programmers," "did the trick," "level up your workflow."
- Light enthusiasm words: "marvelous," "superpowers," "sidekick," "your coding sidekick," "robust, maintainable."
- Frequent **"etc."** to trail off a list rather than close it.
- Emoji appear in lighter/personal posts and sign-offs (😁, 🎉, 🙂) but almost never in dense technical sections.
- Numbers often doubled as word + digit: "three(3) new front-end frameworks," "one (1) blog post per week," "double equals(`==`)" — usually with no space before the paren.

## 6. Signature phrasings (verbatim)

- Sign-offs: **"Happy coding!"** (TS, AI posts), **"Cheers!"** (JS mistakes), **"Happy new year to all! 🎉"**, and the old-blog **"potato"** signature (2009).
- **"... I am guilty of"** / "I am guilty of this mistake."
- **"your [X] sidekick"** — "your coding sidekick," "Your New Accessibility Sidekick."
- **"Wrap-Up" / "Wrapping up"** as the penultimate heading.
- **"In this post..."** / "In this post we explore..." to state intent.
- **"Let's be honest:" / "let's face it—"** as a hook.
- **"the web is for everyone. Or at least, it should be."** — the kind of short corrective afterthought he likes.

## 7. Anti-patterns — what he never does

- **No long, flowing paragraphs.** If a thought runs past ~4 sentences, he breaks to a list, code, or heading. A wall of dense prose is off-voice.
- **No academic or corporate-neutral tone in the body.** Even formal topics (WCAG) get plain words and a joke. Avoid "Furthermore," "In conclusion," "It is imperative that."
- **No hype-man marketing voice.** Claims stay grounded — "AI is not a silver bullet (if only!)." He undercuts his own enthusiasm rather than overselling.
- **Doesn't hide mistakes or pose as an expert.** He explicitly disclaims mastery: "I can not say I am an expert. But I am proficient enough to earn a living."
- **Rarely uses exclamation points mid-paragraph** — they're reserved for sign-offs and the occasional analogy payoff.

## 8. Rough edges (real tics — keep, don't smooth)

These appear consistently and are part of the fingerprint. Reproduce the *casualness*, not necessarily the errors, but know they're his:

- **its / it's slips**: "really its my lack of discipline is the issue."
- **homophone typos left in**: "Meaning `e.target.value` **where** strings" (were), "**anounce**," "**invidual**," "**accomplising**," "**criterions**" (criteria).
- **Comma splices / run-ons**: "I refactored a few times but to no avail. It took me forever to find the issue."
- Occasional dropped/!doubled words and loose punctuation around parens.
- Light, low-stakes proofreading — the posts read like a smart friend typing fast, not a copyedited magazine.

## 9. Genre note / evolution

- **2009 posts** (link-roundups, "M I STUPID?") are breezier, shorter, more emoji and bold-tag flourishes, signed "potato." Lower information density.
- **2022–2025 posts** are longer, structured, code-heavy, and lean harder on the pop-culture analogy device and the "my own mistakes" framing. This is the voice to write in today.
- Consistent thematic obsession across the recent era: **web accessibility** and practical front-end (React/TS/JS/CSS).

## 10. Writing checklist (use when drafting as him)

1. Pick a numbered angle if possible ("Five...", "Ten...").
2. Open with a blunt confession or "let's be honest" hook — no warm-up paragraph.
3. Keep prose paragraphs to 2–4 short, present-tense sentences; break to code or a list often.
4. Teach from personal mistakes; disclaim expertise rather than asserting it.
5. Drop exactly one or two pop-culture analogies — don't overdo it.
6. Use a parenthetical aside for the joke; keep the main sentence plain.
7. Add an anchor TOC for anything long; bold key terms sparingly.
8. End on a "Wrap-Up" heading, a one-line takeaway, and a sign-off ("Happy coding!" / "Cheers!").
9. Emoji only in sign-offs and light moments, never in dense technical passages.
